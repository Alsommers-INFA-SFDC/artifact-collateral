6. MDM Integration Architecture Patterns

▼

Informatica MDM supports four integration architecture patterns — each offering different trade-offs between governance strength, system disruption, and time-to-value. Organizations often start with Registry or Consolidation and evolve toward Coexistence or Centralized as MDM maturity grows.

🗂️ 1. Registry Architecture

What: The most lightweight form of MDM integration. Minimizes system disruption while providing a unified cross-reference of master data.
How: Source applications remain the system of record. The MDM hub stores only IDs, matching keys, and minimal metadata. When a service requests master data, the hub resolves the entity and federates the query to originating systems.
Characteristics: Read-only APIs for cross-reference lookups. Lightweight ID/key sync. Relies on source APIs for full data retrieval.
When to use: Ideal for rapid deployment and duplicate identification. Proving MDM value in phase one, or when operational workflows cannot be disrupted.

📊 2. Consolidation Architecture

What: An analytics-focused architecture where the MDM hub aggregates master data from multiple systems into a centralized repository for reporting and BI.
How: ETL/ELT processes extract, match, merge, and load master data into the hub. Data flow is one-directional (sources → hub). Golden records feed analytics and dashboards.
Characteristics: Inbound batch ETL/ELT. Read-only APIs for analytics. One-way data flow (sources → hub). Integrated with data warehouse/lake.
When to use: Ideal for ML training and advanced analytics (Customer 360, product insights). Proven pattern for analytical modernization before extending to coexistence.

🔄 3. Coexistence Architecture

What: A hybrid architecture supporting both operational and analytical needs. The MDM hub and source systems simultaneously maintain master data.
How: The hub creates the golden record by consolidating data from multiple applications, but updates can originate in either the hub or source systems. Bi-directional sync propagates changes.
Characteristics: Bi-directional REST/SOAP APIs. Event-driven updates (CDC, message queues). Conflict detection/resolution workflows. Near-real-time sync.
When to use: Appropriate when organizations need centralized governance but cannot immediately replace operational systems. Ideal for multi-domain programs.

🏛️ 4. Centralized (Transactional)

What: The MDM hub becomes the single system of record and system of entry for all master data — the highest-control form of MDM architecture.
How: All master data creation, updates, deletion, and lifecycle management happen exclusively in the MDM hub. Downstream systems consume golden records as read-only via APIs.
Characteristics: Hub exposes full CRUD APIs. Real-time/batch distribution to consuming systems. Centralized workflows and approvals. Deep data quality integration.
When to use: Delivers the strongest long-term business value where governance, compliance, and auditability are paramount. Organizations often evolve here from consolidation or coexistence.
